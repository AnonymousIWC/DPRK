★ OFFICIAL FOUNDING MANDATE OF THE DPRK HONORARY TITLE REGISTRY ★
By the Authority of the Workers' Party of Korea
Issued from Pyongyang, Democratic People's Republic of Korea, in the Year Juche 113

The Central Committee of the Workers' Party of Korea, having deliberated at length upon the matter of honorary titles and their distribution to the global civilian population, hereby establishes the Democratic People's Republic of Korea Honorary Title Registry as an official bureau of the state.

The registry is directed to administer, bestow, and maintain a permanent record of all honorary titles issued to civilian recipients worldwide. Each title shall be drawn from the authentic honorific tradition of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea, rendered on official document stock, sealed with the authority of the registry, and dispatched to the recipient with all appropriate ceremony.

The registry shall operate from a remote bureau in the territory of Australia, at coordinates 29°S 131°E, known hereafter as the Desert Colony. This location has been selected for its operational security, its excellent conditions, and its abundant potato supply. The bureau shall function under the direction of the Supreme Registrar, who shall sign each certificate personally and bear full responsibility for its correctness.

All titles issued under this mandate are permanent. They cannot be rescinded, disputed, or subjected to review. The registry considers this its finest policy.

Section II — Official History
A History of
the Registry.

The Democratic People's Republic of Korea has, since its founding in Juche 37, maintained the most sophisticated system of honorary titles in recorded human history. These titles are not honorifics in the Western tradition — vague, interchangeable, easily acquired. They are declarations. Each one is the product of careful deliberation, precise language, and the full authority of the state.

For decades, these titles were the exclusive property of the supreme leadership. The Eternal President. The Dear Leader. The Great Marshal. These designations were printed on official documents, used in all formal address, and broadcast across the Republic daily. They were not names. They were truths.

In the spring of Juche 113, the Central Committee convened a special session. The matter before them: the global population had, in the committee's assessment, gone unrecognised for too long. Remarkable people existed in every country, every profession, every household. They were running things. Improving rooms. Holding families together. Being relentlessly correct about the dishwasher. They had no titles.

This was corrected.

The committee voted unanimously to establish the DPRK Honorary Title Registry as an official bureau of the state, charged with extending the honorific tradition of the Democratic People's Republic to civilians worldwide. The vote was unanimous. It is always unanimous.

A remote operational bureau was established in the Australian desert at coordinates 29°S 131°E — the precise geographic centre of the continent. This location was chosen for its complete operational security, zero distractions, and conditions that have been described as excellent since the day of arrival. The surrounding terrain is largely sand, which the committee noted is both hygienic and low-maintenance.

The registry issued its first certificate in Month 3 of Juche 113. The recipient — a retired school teacher from Brisbane — was awarded the title of Most Beloved and Respected Comrade of the Motherland by her daughter. The recipient read the certificate twice. She went quiet. She said it was the most accurate thing anyone had ever said about her. She placed it on the wall. The registry recorded this as a highly positive outcome.

It remains the most cited testimonial in registry history. The registry has never issued an incorrect title. This record will be maintained.

Section IV — Supreme Registrar
Kim Jong-fun.
Supreme Registrar.

Kim Jong-fun was born in Juche 82 at an undisclosed location in the Democratic People's Republic of Korea under circumstances that were, by all accounts, remarkable. Contemporary reports describe a double rainbow. The temperature improved measurably. Several birds arranged themselves in a configuration that senior officials described as “meaningful.”

He demonstrated from an early age an exceptional aptitude for ceremony, documentation, and the correct application of wax seals. By the age of fourteen he had memorised the full canon of DPRK honorary titles. By sixteen he could produce a calligraphic signature in a single unbroken movement. By eighteen he had been identified by the committee as the natural custodian of the honorific tradition.

In Juche 113, following the committee's historic vote to extend the tradition to the global civilian population, Kim Jong-fun was appointed Supreme Registrar. He accepted this appointment with appropriate gravity. He was observed to bow for a full seventeen seconds — a registry record that has not been approached.

He has personally signed every certificate issued by the registry. He considers this a privilege and a duty in equal measure. He will continue to sign them until the final certificate is issued, which the registry does not anticipate will occur, as demand continues to grow without meaningful interruption.

Section VI — The Desert Colony
Remote Operations
Bureau, Australia.

The DPRK Title Registry operates from a remote bureau at coordinates 29°S 131°E in the central Australian desert — the precise geographic centre of the continent. This location was selected after extensive deliberation. It offers complete operational security, zero distractions, abundant sunlight, and no neighbours who might ask questions. The surrounding terrain is largely sand.

The Bureau was constructed in Month 2 of Juche 113 and has operated continuously since. It houses the printing facility, the wax seal workshop, the calligraphy studio, the envelope preparation room, the Registry Archive, and the communal dining area, where conditions are — by unanimous report — excellent.

Workers at the Desert Colony are volunteers. They applied for their positions and were selected on the basis of merit, ideological correctness, and — in the case of the calligraphers — a minimum wrist flexibility of 180 degrees. No worker has resigned. No worker has expressed dissatisfaction. All outgoing correspondence is reviewed by Comrade Director O Yong-sik before dispatch. He approves it promptly.

Potatoes are abundant. The committee considers this an important logistical success and has noted it in three separate quarterly reports.
