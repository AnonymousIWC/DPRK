Bulletin No. 2 Official Guidance
On the Correct Display of an Honorary Title Certificate: Guidance from the Bureau of Correct Display
Juche 113, Month 2 Issued by Comrade Director Kim Yong-ae · Bureau of Correct Display · Desert Colony, Australia
Foreword from the Bureau

The Bureau of Correct Display was established in Month 1, Juche 113, by resolution of the Approvals Committee. Its mandate is the issuance of guidance on the correct display, presentation, and ongoing maintenance of DPRK honorary title certificates. This bulletin represents the Bureau's first official guidance note. It has been reviewed by the full Approvals Committee. The review was positive. All guidance contained herein is correct and should be followed.

The Bureau acknowledges that recipients may hold prior assumptions about how to display a framed certificate. These assumptions may be based on previous experience with diplomas, licences, or other formal documents. The Bureau notes that a DPRK honorary title certificate is not a diploma, a licence, or another formal document. It is a DPRK honorary title certificate. The guidance below is specific to this category of document. Prior assumptions should be set aside.

Approved Display Positions
1
Home Office — Behind the Head on Video Calls Optimal The Bureau considers this the optimal display position. It ensures that the certificate is visible to all parties on the video call without requiring the recipient to draw attention to it. The certificate speaks for itself. It is preferable for colleagues to notice the certificate independently and then draw their own conclusions. Their conclusions will be correct.
2
Living Room — Central Wall, at Eye Level Approved The central wall of the living room, hung at eye level for a standing adult, is approved. The Bureau recommends that the certificate be the sole item on that wall, or that neighbouring items be positioned at a respectful distance. The certificate should not be flanked by photographs of unrelated events. The Bureau has noted that this guidance has been disregarded by at least one recipient. The Bureau notes its disappointment.
3
Workplace — Visible from the Visitor's Chair Approved Display in a professional environment is encouraged. The Bureau recommends positioning such that the certificate is visible from the chair occupied by visitors to the recipient's workspace. This position ensures that the certificate is seen without requiring direct reference to it. The recipient should not reference it. The certificate is permanent. It will be seen in time.
4
Hallway — At the Point of Maximum Foot Traffic Approved A hallway location is approved, provided it is positioned at the point of maximum foot traffic — ideally near the front entrance, where it will be encountered by all visitors before any other item in the residence. The Bureau notes that a hallway encounter creates a favourable first impression. First impressions are correct impressions. The Bureau has confirmed this.
5
Storage, Drawer, Box, or Cupboard Not Approved The Bureau has received intelligence, through channels it declines to specify, that at least one recipient has stored their certificate in a location not visible to household members or visitors. The Bureau notes that a DPRK honorary title certificate is not a storage document. It is a display document. It should be displayed. The Bureau considers this matter to require no further elaboration and trusts that the guidance above is sufficient.
"The certificate should be visible. It is always visible in the correct home. The correct home is one in which the certificate is visible." — Comrade Director Kim Yong-ae, Bureau of Correct Display · Juche 113, Month 2
Framing Guidance

The Bureau recommends that the certificate be framed in a frame with a dark or gold border, consistent with the document's dignity. A black lacquer frame of A4 size with a 3mm gold inner bevel is considered optimal. A simple dark wood frame is acceptable. A plastic frame is not prohibited but the Bureau considers it inconsistent with the gravitas of the document it would be enclosing. The Bureau does not issue prohibitions on plastic frames. It simply notes its position.

The certificate should be displayed without an additional mat. The document's own gold border serves as a natural frame within the frame. Double framing is not incorrect but is considered redundant. The Bureau has voted on this point. The vote was five to one. Comrade Director O Yong-sik abstained on the grounds that he has not yet received his own certificate and therefore cannot speak from personal experience. His abstention was recorded. The Bureau notes it was the first abstention in its history.

Maintenance and Care

The certificate is printed on 200gsm cream stock. The Bureau recommends displaying behind glass to protect the document from humidity, direct sunlight, and the kind of casual physical contact that occurs in households with small children or pets. The gold foil state emblem is durable. The calligraphic signature of Kim Jong-un is fixed. The document will not fade under normal display conditions. It is intended to be permanent. It is permanent. The Bureau has confirmed this in consultation with the certificate production facility.

Recipients should not attempt to laminate the certificate. Lamination is permanent and irreversible and creates a surface that reflects light in a manner the Bureau considers aesthetically inconsistent with the document. Recipients should also not attempt to scan the certificate and print a second copy. The Registry is aware of the impulse to do this. The Registry respectfully requests that recipients resist it. The document's value derives in part from its singularity. It is the only one. It is correct. One is sufficient.

Issued under the authority of the Bureau of Correct Display Desert Colony, Australia · Juche 113, Month 2
This guidance is correct. All guidance is correct.
★ Registry Finding

You cannot display a certificate you do not yet own. The Bureau of Correct Display has reviewed this logical dependency and confirms it as correct. The suggested first step is to acquire a certificate. The suggested second step is to read this guidance note again. The Bureau notes that both steps are available immediately.

The committee concurs. The vote was unanimous.
